01 · The problem

Local AI Agents Are Powerful,
But Not Reliable by Default

Qwen, Llama, Gemma, Mistral, Phi — run any of these locally and the gap to a hosted frontier model is obvious within a few prompts. It's tempting to blame the model. Usually that's not the real problem.

Weak answer

A small local model asked a specific question about your data answers in generalities, or confidently makes something up.

Real cause

The model was never wrong about what it knows — it simply never had your private, contextual data in front of it.

06 · Retrieval-augmented generation

The Full RAG Flow

Every chat turn that needs a highlight repeats these seven steps — two different local models do two very different jobs, and this exact path never leaves the machine.

    G --> H["Return grounded

Important nuance

mxbai-embed-large is a general-purpose embedding model — trained on broad web text, not fine-tuned on books, highlights, or this app's data. It's a word/meaning-similarity finder: good at "these two sentences are about the same thing," not at literary understanding. Step B only narrows down which highlights are worth reading; step G's chat model (qwen3.5:4b) still does all of the actual reasoning over them.

This specific flow never calls out to the internet — embedding, search, prompt assembly, and generation all happen on the local stack.

One real exception, elsewhere

A separate tool, GenerateBookContext, does leave the machine: BookContextService calls OpenLibraryService, which makes two real HTTPS requests to openlibrary.org for a book's synopsis. It's not a cloud AI API — just a free, unauthenticated metadata lookup — and it's cached on Book.Synopsis, so it runs once per book, ever, not on every turn. Everything downstream of it (prompt building, generation) is still local.

07 · What actually happened, no retrieval

Four Real Attempts, Zero Retrieval

Same local model, asked directly — no embeddings, no pgvector. The outcome depended entirely on whether the title happened to be in the model's training memory.

>>> Talk about the book Jennifer Government
Failed · safety paranoia

Never answered. Treated "Government" as a political red flag, suspected a jailbreak attempt, and spiraled into checking Chinese political sensitivity — for a Western satirical novel.

"This is a common technique to bypass content filters or to explore a topic under a different guise."
>>> ...written by Max Berry
Failed · infinite loop

Adding the author didn't help. It cross-checked invented near-misses in a repeating self-correction loop, then was cut off mid-thought without ever answering.

"Wait, is it possible the user is referring to 'The Government of Jennifer'?"
>>> The World Jones Made, by Philip K. Dick
Failed · same loop pattern

A different, less-famous PKD novel hits the identical failure mode. This isn't about one title — it's about anything outside the model's confident training memory.

>>> Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?, by PKD
Succeeded · after ~5 minutes

Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep? by Philip K. Dick was published in 1968. The novel follows a bounty hunter named Rick Deckard whose job is to dispose of rogue androids. Unlike humans, androids are often called replicants.

The book centers on the concept of empathy and what it means to be human. Deckard uses a "mirror" to measure this feeling. As he investigates a murder, he realizes he begins to lose his humanity. He struggles to distinguish between a machine and a human being. The story is set in a near-future, desolate world where animals are scarce.

Dick wrote it as a philosophical exploration rather than a simple action story. It questions whether machines can feel more than their creators. The ending leaves the nature of the protagonist and the reality of the events open for the reader to decide. The book remains a standalone work that focuses on the psychological depth of its themes.

08 · Grounded with retrieval

Same Book, Now It Just Answers

Wire in embedding-based title lookup, the GenerateBookContext tool, and a real Open Library synopsis — and the same question that previously broke down resolves immediately, no author hint needed.

>>> Talk about the book Jennifer Government
Succeeded · ~30 seconds

In Jennifer Government, Max Barry paints a dystopian vision of a future where the United States has been entirely privatized, reflecting the anxieties of the post-9/11 era. The novel explores themes of surveillance, the erosion of civil liberties, and the rise of corporate power over essential services. Set in a world where the line between public and private spheres has blurred, the story follows a man caught in a murder case within this oppressive landscape, offering a cautionary tale about the consequences of unchecked corporate expansion.

The book is deeply rooted in the speculative fiction and cyberpunk movements, capturing the cultural zeitgeist of the early 21st century. It serves as a critique of deregulation and the privatization of essential services, highlighting the fragility of democracy in the face of unchecked corporate power.

No political-risk spiral this time — with a real synopsis already in the prompt, the model never treated "Government" as a red flag to investigate. ~30 seconds, start to finish, versus the open-ended loop (and the unprompted detour into Chinese political sensitivity) seen without retrieval.

09 · The constraint

The Context Window Problem

This stack runs qwen3.5:4b with Ollama:NumCtx = 8192 tokens. That budget has to hold the system prompt, the conversation, the question, and any retrieved context — for every single turn.

Can't do

Dump every book and every highlight into the prompt and hope the model finds the right one.

Must do

Rank candidates by relevance first, then send only what fits the remaining budget.

Already configurable

BookNotes:TopKRelevantNotes (default 20) and BookNotes:MaxAnalysisNotes (default 50) exist precisely to cap this.

10 · Measuring & spending the budget

Token Counting Keeps the Window Honest

// TokenAccumulator, observed per turn
TotalInputTokensProcessed
TotalOutputTokensGenerated
MaxPromptTokens ← the one that matters
ModelCallCount

// the number shown in the UI ring
ContextUsagePct = MaxPromptTokens * 100 / NumCtx

A single turn can trigger several internal model calls (reasoning, tool call, final answer). Summing them overstates pressure — so the app tracks the largest single prompt, not the sum, and renders it as a Shoelace progress ring next to the chat input.

The same signal generalizes into a budgeting pattern: reserve tokens for the system prompt and the question, reserve tokens for the answer, and spend whatever is left on retrieved context.

More retrieved context generally improves grounding. It also costs latency. Summarizing retrieved context can shrink it back down, at the price of an extra processing step.

Illustrative figures. Summarization is a deliberate next step in this project — it is named and explicitly deferred in the context-token-awareness and context-pressure-metrics specs, not yet wired into the running app.

11 · Takeaways

Reliability Is Architecture, Not a Model Setting

A local model is not unreliable because it's local. It's unreliable when nothing around it does the work of finding, sizing, and budgeting the right context.

Embeddings

Turn meaning into coordinates pgvector can search.

Retrieval

Find the highlights that actually matter to this question.

Vector database

pgvector keeps the search inside Postgres, not a separate service.

Context selection

Cap what gets sent — TopK and MaxAnalysisNotes exist for this.

Token counting

Know how full the context window is before Ollama does.

Summarization

Shrink retrieved context further when the token budget gets tight (next step).

Prompt construction

Assemble system, profile, and retrieved context into one grounded prompt.

Real-world grounding

Open Library's free API supplies a real synopsis the model can't hallucinate.

All of the above, together — no single piece makes a local agent reliable. The grounded answer shown earlier is what that combination looks like in practice.

12 · What's left

The Next Bottleneck Is Hardware

Once the architecture is sound, the ceiling moves to the machine running it.

Today

A 4B chat model and an 8K context window are deliberate choices — they fit on consumer GPUs (this stack passes through /dev/dri and /dev/kfd with OLLAMA_VULKAN=1).

This machine · Radeon 680M-class iGPU · ~30 GiB unified RAM (512 MiB VRAM + ~15.3 GiB GTT)
The limit

Bigger models and longer context windows need more VRAM and memory bandwidth than most local machines have.

Watching

Local-AI-focused hardware — AMD's Ryzen AI platforms, NVIDIA's DGX Spark-class boxes — points at where this ceiling moves next. Early days; worth watching, not betting on yet.

What to watch

AMD's Max+ chips bring a large shared memory pool to ordinary mini-PCs and laptops.

NVIDIA's DGX Spark commits an entire dedicated box to the same idea, at a larger scale.

Both point toward more private, on-device AI workflows.

Curious what VRAM a given model size actually needs on your own hardware?

Try the VRAM calculator

An LLM's weights and its growing KV cache (the per-token memory cost of context) both have to fit in GPU-addressable memory at once. Run out, and the model either won't load, or spills into far slower system memory. That's the entire reason both of these platforms are built around one giant shared memory pool instead of a small, separate VRAM chip.

GPU-addressable memory across this machine, two current discrete gaming GPUs, and the three unified-memory platforms above. The Legion Go S figure is this device's own measured split (512 MiB fixed VRAM + ~15.3 GiB GTT). Model-size figures are an illustrative Q4-quantization rule of thumb, not a benchmark.

13 · Beyond books

The Same Pattern, Other Domains

Behavioral embeddings

Nubank-style fraud and recommendation systems embed transactions and user behavior, not just text. Similar spending or usage patterns land close together in the same vector space.

Semantic search

A natural-language search bar over internal docs or support tickets. For many internal tools, pgvector inside the database you already run is a simpler starting point than standing up Elasticsearch.
